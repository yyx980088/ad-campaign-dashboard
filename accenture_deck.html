The insight: High CTR ≠ high revenue. These audiences aren't competing — they play different roles. Male segments drive efficient reach; Women 18-34 drive conversion. The strategy uses both, for different jobs.
The Full-Funnel Strategy
Top of Funnel
Awareness — Male Segments (45-54, 35-44, 18-24)
CTR 32–33% → cost-efficient reach. Broad-reach formats built for impressions and clicks, not conversion.
Mid Funnel
Consideration — Festive demand windows
Ramp up 4–6 weeks ahead of Hari Raya (Mar–Apr) and Deepavali/year-end (Aug–Sep) peaks — not in reaction to them.
Bottom of Funnel
Conversion — Women 18-24 & Women 25-34
Highest cumulative revenue. Retargeting, shoppable posts, festive UGC — optimised to close the sale.
Why this works: Every dollar has a defined role tied to evidence — not intuition. The funnel structure resolves what would otherwise look like a contradiction in the data.

Seasonality — A Pattern the Current Plan Isn't Exploiting

Data Evidence

Two demand peaks emerge clearly — consistent with how people actually shop for interior décor.

MonthImpressionsRevenueSignal
Apr 2125,000$1,900Peak 1
May 2017,000$1,500Trough
Sep 1626,000$2,000Peak 2 · Best month
Oct 1518,000$1,500Trough
Real-world alignment: The Mar–Apr peak aligns closely with Hari Raya Aidilfitri, Malaysia's largest festive season — a period where home redecoration, new furnishings, and full interior refreshes are deeply embedded in open house (rumah terbuka) culture. The Sep peak likely reflects early year-end festive preparation, ahead of Deepavali (typically Oct–Nov) and the broader year-end hosting and gifting season.
The opportunity: Re-timing a portion of low-season spend to arrive ahead of each demand peak captures more revenue without committing new budget — same dollars, better placed.
What I'm not yet confident about: This is based on a single annual cycle. One year isn't enough to call this statistically seasonal vs. coincidental. Phase 1 of the roadmap (re-timing only, no new creative) is deliberately low-commitment so we can test the hypothesis before betting the full plan on it.

HOW (1/2): Execution & Rollout

Execution · Implementation
What — Creative by Funnel Role
Top of Funnel — Male Segments (Awareness)
  • Format: Short-form video, "shop the look" carousels, broad-reach display
  • Creative: Aspirational interiors styled for Raya open houses and year-end hosting
Bottom of Funnel — Women 18-34 (Conversion)
  • Format: Shoppable posts, retargeting with bundle offers
  • Creative: UGC "before and after" Raya and festive home transformations
Safeguard: Creative rotation built in from day one — avoids audience fatigue and CPC creep as spend concentrates.
When — 90-Day Phased Rollout
Phase 1
Re-time existing spend — no new creative
Shift May/Oct budget into Feb/Aug ahead of Raya and year-end peaks. Lowest-risk move: same dollars, better timing.
0–30 days
Phase 2
Launch funnel-split creative
Awareness formats to male segments; conversion formats (shoppable, UGC, retargeting) to Women 18-34. Review Phase 1 read first.
30–60 days

Retest Women 35-44 — close the open question
Two consistent campaigns with fresh creative. Decide to scale or retire based on segment ROI data.
60–90 days
Each phase is a checkpoint — no commitment to the next step until the current one generates a read.

Appendix — Anticipated Questions

Q&A Prep

Have this slide ready but don't present it unprompted. Use only if challenged.

"What if Women 35-44 is a strategic priority for the client regardless of the data?"
Then the retest becomes even more important — we want to find the creative or targeting angle that works for them, not deprioritise a segment the client has committed to. The data tells us something isn't landing yet, not that the audience is wrong.
"How would you validate seasonality isn't a coincidence with only one year of data?"
I'd want at least one more annual cycle before betting the full plan on it — which is exactly why Phase 1 only re-times existing spend rather than committing new creative budget to a seasonal assumption we can't yet verify.
"What if the client just wants more reach — not this funnel segmentation?"
I'd ask what "success" means to them beyond reach — because the data shows reach and revenue come from different audiences here. I'd rather align on the business objective first than optimise the wrong metric well. Reach that doesn't convert is a cost, not a result.
"You mentioned $100-150 incremental revenue — how did you get to that number?"
Directional estimate: ~20% of trough-month spend is ~$60. At observed conversion rates in the April/September campaigns (highest ~$1,900–2,000 revenue at ~$230-238 cost), that additional $60 at similar efficiency would generate roughly $100-150 in revenue. It's an assumption-based estimate, not a forecast — I'd revisit after one cycle of actual data.
