Client Overview

Context

The client is an interior décor brand running a 10-month national paid digital campaign — a category where purchase decisions are driven by life moments and seasonal cycles, not continuous demand.

  • Campaign window: January – October (10 data points)
  • Audience scope: Mixed gender, ages 18–54 across four age bands
  • True objective: Convert ad spend into purchase revenue — efficiently, not just visibly
  • Category behaviour: Interior décor tracks closely to home-life events — moving, spring refreshes, pre-holiday styling
Why the objective framing matters: Optimising for reach or CTR alone without anchoring to revenue conversion would actively mislead strategy. Every recommendation here is graded on whether it moves the business outcome — not just the campaign metric.
A note on the data: We have 10 campaign data points, each pairing a single month with a single demographic segment. Several segments appear only once. I'll flag explicitly where this limits confidence.

The insight: Optimising purely for CTR (male segments at 32-33%) would direct budget away from the audiences generating the most cumulative revenue (Women 18-34 at $3,500-3,600). One metric alone actively misleads the strategy.
What this unlocks: These two groups aren't competing — they serve different funnel roles. Male segments are efficient at generating reach and clicks. Female 18-34 segments are efficient at converting that engagement into revenue. The strategy should use both, for different jobs.
3

Strategy — A Full-Funnel Approach Built on That Insight

Recommendation

Rather than one generic campaign to everyone, restructure spend so each audience is doing the job it's statistically best suited for.

Top of Funnel
Awareness & Reach — Male Segments (45-54, 35-44, 18-24)
CTR 32–33% → cost-efficient traffic generation. Broad-reach formats optimised for impressions and clicks, not conversion. Goal: build the pipeline.
Mid Funnel
Consideration — Seasonal demand windows (Mar–Apr and Aug–Sep)
Category demand peaks in spring and early fall. Ramp up full-funnel activity 4–6 weeks ahead of each peak — not in reaction to it.
Bottom of Funnel
Conversion — Women 18-24 & Women 25-34
$3,500–3,600 cumulative revenue, $1,750–1,800 average per campaign. Conversion-optimised formats, retargeting, promotional bundles. Goal: close the sale.
Why this works: Every dollar now has a defined role tied to evidence — not demographic intuition or a single metric ranking. The funnel structure also explains what would otherwise look like a contradiction in the data.

Seasonality — A Pattern the Current Plan Isn't Exploiting

Data Evidence

Two demand peaks emerge clearly — consistent with how people actually shop for interior décor.

Real-world alignment: The Mar–Apr peak aligns closely with Hari Raya Aidilfitri, Malaysia's largest festive season — a period where home redecoration, new furnishings, and full interior refreshes are deeply embedded in open house (rumah terbuka) culture. The Sep peak likely reflects early year-end festive preparation, ahead of Deepavali (typically Oct–Nov) and the broader year-end hosting and gifting season.
The opportunity: Re-timing a portion of low-season spend to arrive ahead of each demand peak captures more revenue without committing new budget — same dollars, better placed.
What I'm not yet confident about: This is based on a single annual cycle. One year isn't enough to call this statistically seasonal vs. coincidental. Phase 1 of the roadmap (re-timing only, no new creative) is deliberately low-commitment so we can test the hypothesis before betting the full plan on it.

Roadmap — How I'd Sequence This Over 90 Days

Implementation

All three moves are sequenced so each phase generates a read before the next phase commits further budget. Nothing here is irreversible.

Phase 1
Re-time existing spend — no new creative required
Shift a portion of May/October budget earlier into February/August, ahead of the seasonal demand peaks. Lowest-risk move: same dollars, different timing. Fast to execute, fast to read.
0–30 days
Phase 2
Launch funnel-role split — awareness vs. conversion creative
Awareness creative (video/carousel) to male segments. Conversion creative (shoppable, retargeting, UGC) to Women 18-34. Review Phase 1 timing data before full commitment.
30–60 days
Phase 3
Close the open question — controlled retest of Women 35-44
Run two consistent campaigns for the segment with fresh creative. Review segment-level ROI across all active segments. Decide whether Women 35-44 earns more budget or gets retired. Adjust Phase 4 annual plan accordingly.
60–90 days
Each phase has a built-in checkpoint. If Phase 1 re-timing doesn't move the needle, we pause before investing in new creative. The sequencing protects the budget while generating the evidence needed to commit to each next step.

Risks & Mitigations

Risk Management

Naming risks upfront is part of the recommendation — not a caveat to it.

⚡
CPC creep from concentrated spend in fewer segments
Mitigation: Maintain a 10–15% "exploration" budget continuously testing adjacent segments (e.g., Men 25-34, which posted strong $1,900 single-campaign revenue) — keeps us from being locked into one lane as inventory competition rises.
😴
Creative fatigue from repeated exposure to a narrower audience
Mitigation: Build a rotation cadence into the content calendar from day one. Flag creative refresh triggers at reach/frequency thresholds, not calendar dates.
📅
Seasonality pattern may not repeat (one annual cycle only)
Mitigation: Phase 1 is fully reversible — we re-time existing dollars rather than committing new ones. If the pattern doesn't repeat, we reverse the timing with no sunk creative cost.
🎯
Client's strategic priorities may differ from pure ROI optimisation
Mitigation: These recommendations are grounded in available data — but if a segment is strategically important for brand or pipeline reasons beyond this dataset, the retest approach (Phase 3) applies: find what works for that audience, rather than deprioritising them.

Appendix — Anticipated Questions

Q&A Prep

Have this slide ready but don't present it unprompted. Use only if challenged.

"What if Women 35-44 is a strategic priority for the client regardless of the data?"
Then the retest becomes even more important — we want to find the creative or targeting angle that works for them, not deprioritise a segment the client has committed to. The data tells us something isn't landing yet, not that the audience is wrong.
"How would you validate seasonality isn't a coincidence with only one year of data?"
I'd want at least one more annual cycle before betting the full plan on it — which is exactly why Phase 1 only re-times existing spend rather than committing new creative budget to a seasonal assumption we can't yet verify.
"What if the client just wants more reach — not this funnel segmentation?"
I'd ask what "success" means to them beyond reach — because the data shows reach and revenue come from different audiences here. I'd rather align on the business objective first than optimise the wrong metric well. Reach that doesn't convert is a cost, not a result.
"You mentioned $100-150 incremental revenue — how did you get to that number?"
Directional estimate: ~20% of trough-month spend is ~$60. At observed conversion rates in the April/September campaigns (highest ~$1,900–2,000 revenue at ~$230-238 cost), that additional $60 at similar efficiency would generate roughly $100-150 in revenue. It's an assumption-based estimate, not a forecast — I'd revisit after one cycle of actual data.
